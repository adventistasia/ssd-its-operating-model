# Delivery Owner Final Reflection

Status: working draft
Perspective: Delivery Owner / Project Manager

## 1. Overall judgment

### Was the framework usable in practice for this request?

Yes, but only if the Delivery Owner is willing to escalate when the control surface gets broader than the visible task.

The framework stopped me from treating this as a simple tracker request. It forced the right questions about source ownership, acceptance, privacy, and support.

### What parts helped most?

- starting at Stage 1 when the stage was unclear
- the distinction between a Work Brief and a fuller initiative baseline
- the insistence on named owners and explicit acceptance
- the ability to keep source records owned by HR and the Committee Secretary while still building one combined view

### What parts were hardest to apply?

- deciding when the work stopped being small
- handling split acceptance without making the scenario feel over-engineered
- deciding how much detail belongs in Stage 2 versus Stage 4
- keeping the solution visible enough for governance without turning it into a heavy project pack

## 2. Assumptions and friction

### What assumptions had to be made?

- Angie can act as sponsor and delegated decision authority for office operations
- HR can allow controlled leave-status visibility without giving up ownership
- the Committee Secretary can expose itinerary authority and revisions in a controlled way
- the first useful release can be a visibility layer over existing records

### What created delivery friction?

- multiple authoritative owners
- privacy questions around leave data
- revision rules for itinerary records
- the risk that a tracker becomes a false system of record
- the framework makes the Delivery Owner spell out split acceptance rather than gloss over it

### What reduced ambiguity?

- making the source owners visible instead of hidden
- explicitly saying the tracker is not the source of truth
- using an Initiative Definition Document instead of a Work Brief
- documenting the rules, dependencies, and readiness controls separately

## 3. Weight, vagueness, and rollout

### What felt too heavy?

- a full project-level artifact set would have been too much for a staff tracker request
- even the medium-complexity path still required discipline to keep the work from growing past its purpose

### What felt too vague?

- the exact threshold for Work Brief versus Initiative Definition Document
- the exact level of source data freshness needed before the tracker is trustworthy
- how far the delivery team can go before the support owner must be fixed

### What should change before wider rollout?

- add a sharper small-work escalation guide with examples like this one
- add clearer guidance for split acceptance across business, records, and privacy owners
- add a clearer rule for when a visibility layer becomes too integration-heavy for a small governed item

### Would the Delivery Owner use this framework again for similar work? Why or why not?

Yes. It is better than improvising, especially when the work sounds simple but has real governance implications.

I would use it again because it exposed the real control questions early. I would not use it as a mechanical checklist without judgment, because that would produce too much ceremony for the wrong kinds of work.

## 4. Final candid answer

The framework works best as a control spine.

For this request, it helped me:

- slow down enough to ask for the real owners
- choose a proportionate but governed path
- keep the solution from becoming a shadow record
- stop at execution readiness instead of pretending delivery had already happened

That is the right outcome for this simulation.
