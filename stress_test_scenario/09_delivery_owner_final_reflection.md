# Delivery Owner Final Reflection

Status: working draft  
Perspective: Delivery Owner / Project Manager

## 1. Overall judgment

### Was the framework usable in practice for this request?

Yes, with reservations.

It was usable enough to stop me from guessing, to start at Stage 1, and to justify a scaled path instead of a full project pack. It was not usable in a purely mechanical way. I still had to make judgment calls about what "small but governed" means when ownership is ambiguous.

### What parts helped most?

- The instruction to start at Stage 1 when the current stage is unclear.
- The distinction between small governed work and a fuller initiative path.
- The availability of the [Work Brief](../work_delivery/work_brief/work_brief_specification.md) as a lighter control record.
- The repeated insistence on naming owners, decision authority, and acceptance basis.
- The reminder that work should not quietly start because it feels urgent.

### What parts were hardest to apply?

- Determining exactly how much Stage 1 assessment is enough for a request this small.
- Working out how to handle split authority between office usefulness and official-record correctness.
- Deciding whether leave and itinerary status belong in one work item without forcing a larger scope than the requestor intended.
- Knowing when the Work Brief is enough for the technical team and when a small support artifact is also needed.

## 2. Assumptions and friction

### What assumptions had to be made?

- that an office operations leadership role would likely sponsor the work
- that leave records already exist and are separately owned
- that the first useful release can be a reporting view rather than a workflow engine
- that Microsoft 365 is an available and proportionate platform direction

### What created delivery friction?

- no explicit sponsor
- no explicit Outcome Owner
- no explicit Acceptance Authority
- no clear answer on whether usefulness acceptance and record-correctness acceptance are the same thing
- tension between Jane's desire for simplicity and the Committee Secretary's need for formal correctness
- the framework does not itself resolve cross-record source-of-truth questions

### What reduced ambiguity?

- the Committee Secretary's insistence that the official itinerary record remains controlling
- the programmer's demand for a system-of-record decision before build
- the Work Brief path, which gave me a place to keep scope, ownership, risks, and decisions visible without escalating to a full initiative definition

## 3. Weight, vagueness, and rollout

### What felt too heavy?

- running three Stage 1 artifacts for a request that obviously feels small can feel heavy unless the Delivery Owner keeps them very short
- there is a real risk that practitioners will over-document small work because the framework is rich in structure

### What felt too vague?

- the exact threshold for when a Work Brief is sufficient versus when a fuller initiative record is needed
- how to handle split acceptance ownership in practice
- how much supporting definition detail is acceptable before Stage 3 without drifting into Stage 4 behavior

### What should change before wider rollout?

- add a sharper small-work decision aid with explicit examples
- add guidance for split acceptance models, especially where data correctness and business usefulness belong to different roles
- add a very small-work assessment pattern that keeps Stage 1 visible without making it feel ceremonial
- add clearer guidance on source-of-truth conflicts when a solution combines multiple official record sets

### Would the Delivery Owner use this framework again for similar work? Why or why not?

Yes, but only if I could apply it pragmatically.

I would use it again because it prevented a sloppy jump from request to build and forced the right questions into view. I would not use it as a rigid template checklist for every small office request, because that would likely create resistance faster than it creates clarity.

## 4. Final candid answer

The framework works best as a navigation spine, not as a substitute for judgment.

For this request, it helped me:

- slow down just enough
- choose a proportionate path
- expose missing ownership
- protect the work from a naive "just build a tracker" response

It struggled when I needed exact operational guidance for a small item with mixed record authority. That is where the framework was still too interpretive and where a less experienced Delivery Owner would probably hesitate or improvise.
