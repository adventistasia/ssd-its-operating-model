# Delivery Owner Final Reflection

Status: working draft  
Perspective: Delivery Owner / Project Manager

## 1. Overall judgment

Yes, the framework was usable in practice, with reservations.

It was useful enough to stop me from guessing, to start at Stage 1, and to justify a full initiative path instead of forcing the work into a Work Brief. It was not usable in a purely mechanical way. I still had to make judgment calls about what small means once the request exposes multiple authoritative sources, role-based update rules, and audit concerns.

## 2. What helped most

- the instruction to start at Stage 1 when the current stage is unclear
- the distinction between small governed work and a fuller initiative path
- the ability to scale up to an Initiative Definition Document when the work is too broad or control-sensitive
- the repeated insistence on naming owners, decision rights, and acceptance basis
- the reminder that work should not quietly start because it feels urgent

## 3. What was hardest to apply

- deciding exactly when the request stopped being a simple tracker and became a custom app initiative
- handling split authority between itinerary correctness, leave correctness, and business usefulness
- deciding how much Stage 2 detail is enough before the work becomes design-heavy
- knowing when execution readiness is real versus merely optimistic

## 4. Assumptions and friction

Assumptions that had to be made:

- Angie was a real sponsor candidate willing to back the work if governance was explicit
- official itinerary and leave records already exist separately
- the first useful release should be a controlled app, not a spreadsheet
- IT Operations will eventually own support

Delivery friction:

- no confirmed sponsor at intake
- no confirmed Outcome Owner at intake
- no confirmed Acceptance Authority at intake
- no agreed source-of-truth hierarchy at intake
- no agreed rule for status precedence at intake
- tension between Jane's desire for simplicity and the control owners' need for auditability

What reduced ambiguity:

- the Committee Secretary's insistence that approved itinerary records remain controlling
- the HR leave owner's insistence that leave status stay owned and private
- the programmer's demand for rule clarity before build
- the sponsor's willingness to fund the work if governance was explicit
- the framework's ability to make the work progress through staged governance rather than jump to execution

## 5. Weight, vagueness, and rollout

What felt too heavy:

- Stage 1 can feel heavy if the artifacts are written like formal project packs instead of short governance records
- the framework exposes a lot of discipline for a request that began as a simple office convenience

What felt too vague:

- the exact threshold for when a Work Brief is enough versus when a fuller initiative record is required
- how to express split acceptance in a practical way
- how to mark a work item as ready for execution when support ownership is still maturing

What should change before wider rollout:

- add sharper examples for when a small request becomes a full initiative
- add guidance for split acceptance models where business usefulness, source correctness, privacy, and support ownership are separate concerns
- add an example of a custom app path that clearly crosses the Work Brief boundary
- add a small control checklist for source precedence and auditability issues

Would I use this framework again for similar work? Yes, but only if I keep treating it as a navigation spine rather than a rigid checklist.

## 6. Final candid answer

The framework works best when it tells me where the work sits, what control points matter, when to stop, when to scale up, and when to require ownership before execution.

For this request, it pushed the work into a custom app build path for good reasons:

- multiple authoritative sources
- frequent itinerary revisions
- role-based update rules
- status precedence logic
- auditability and records concerns
- sponsor willingness if governance is clear

The right stop point for this scenario is execution readiness, not actual execution. That is the point where the work is governable, but the folder still stays honest about the fact that no build or deployment happened here.
